At the end of class on Wednesday of week 9, we defined a regular surface as a subset of \(S \subsetneq \mathbb{R}^3\) such that for each point \(p \in S\), there exists:

  • an open neighborhood \(U\) around \(p\) (with respect to the topology on \(S\)),
  • an open subset \(V \subseteq \mathbb{R}^2\), and
  • a continuous smooth (i.e. infinitely differentiable) bijection \(\mathbf{f}\colon V \to U\) such that \(\mathbf{f}_x(\mathbf{v})\) and \(\mathbf{f}_y(\mathbf{v})\) are linearly independent for all $\mathbf{v} \in V$.
  1. [2] Show that every plane $Ax + By + Cz = D$ in $\mathbb{R}^3$ is a regular surface by giving an explicit parameterization \(\mathbf{f}\).
  2. [2] The double-cone not a regular surface. Let the map \( \mathbf{f}\colon (-1,1) \times (0, \pi) \to \mathbb{R}^3 \) be defined as \[ \mathbf{f}(r, \theta) = \begin{pmatrix}r\cos\theta\\r\sin\theta\\r\end{pmatrix}. \]
    1. Is $\mathbf{f}$ a continuous smooth bijection onto the surface?
    2. Are \(\mathbf{f}_x(\mathbf{v})\) and \(\mathbf{f}_y(\mathbf{v})\) are linearly independent for all $\mathbf{v} \in V$?
    3. Do either of the above facts prove that the cone is not a regular surface?
  3. [5] Prove that the cone (or the double-cone) is not a regular surface by proving that there is no neighborhood around the tip that have the necessary properties.
  4. [3+3] We say that a map \(\mathbb{R}^k \to \mathbb{R}^m\) is $n$-times differentiable (denoted $C^n$) if we can take all derivatives of order $n$ and they result in a continuous function. For example, if \(f\colon\mathbb{R}^3 \to \mathbb{R}^4\) is $C^2$, then \(f_x\), \(f_y\), \(f_{xx}\), \(f_{xy}\), \(f_{yx}\), and \(f_{yx}\) are all continuous functions. If we can take infinitely many derivatives, we say that the map is $C^\infty$ or smooth.
    1. [3] Use induction to prove that if $f$ and $g$ are smooth, then their composition $f \circ g$ is smooth.
    2. [3] Prove that the bump function \(\psi\colon \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}\) given by \[ \psi(x) = \begin{cases} \exp(1/(x^2-1)) & x \in (-1,1) \\ 0 & x \in (-\infty,-1] \cup [1, \infty) \end{cases} \] is smooth.
